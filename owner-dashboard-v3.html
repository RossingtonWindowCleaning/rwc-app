How to file your Self Assessment
Step-by-step guide for end of tax year
›
Filing your Self Assessment
Key deadlines
5 April — Tax year ends
5 October — Register for Self Assessment if first year
31 October — Paper return deadline
31 January — Online return + payment deadline
31 July — Second payment on account (if applicable)
Step 1 — Register (first year only)

Go to gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment and register as a sole trader. You'll get a UTR (Unique Taxpayer Reference) by post within 10 working days. Keep this safe — you'll need it every year.

Step 2 — Gather your figures

Open your TRANSACTIONS sheet and use the P&L SUMMARY tab to get your totals for the tax year. You need:

  • Total income (all INCOME rows)
  • Total expenses by category (each EXPENSE category summed)
  • Vehicle costs (separate fuel from insurance, MOT, repairs)
Step 3 — Log into HMRC

Go to gov.uk/log-in-file-self-assessment-tax-return. Sign in with your Government Gateway ID. If you don't have one, create one — you'll need your UTR and National Insurance number.

Step 4 — Fill in the SA103 (self-employment) section

This is where your transaction data goes. Map your spreadsheet to these boxes:

Box 9 (Turnover) — Your total INCOME
Box 10 (Other income) — Usually £0
Box 11–19 (Expenses) — Your EXPENSE totals grouped by HMRC category
Box 20 (Net profit) — Income minus expenses
Step 5 — Cash basis vs Traditional accounting

Most window cleaners use cash basis — record income when received, expenses when paid. This matches how this app logs things. Tick the cash basis box on your return.

Step 6 — Pay your tax

Once submitted, HMRC will tell you how much to pay. Pay by 31 January via:

  • Bank transfer (fastest)
  • Direct debit
  • Online banking app

If you owe more than £1,000, HMRC will also ask for "payments on account" toward next year's tax — half by 31 January, half by 31 July.

Tips
  • Keep all receipts (paper or photo) for 5 years after submission
  • Set aside ~25% of profit each month into a separate tax savings account
  • If unsure, pay an accountant — usually £150–£300 for a sole trader return, fully tax-deductible
  • Don't leave it until January — start your return in May, takes the pressure off

This guide is general information only, not personal tax advice. For complex situations consult HMRC or a qualified accountant.
